Introduction

"Let that sink in" Elon Musk posted, right after he bought Twitter for 42 billion US-dollars, and attached a photo carrying a bathroom sink into the headquarters, while throwing a goofy grin at the camera (source). With the entry of the self-proclaimed free speech advocate, political polarisation has reached a new peak (source) in social media. This trend of deregulating social media platforms has taken another turn after Trump being elected, placing Musk into the administration (source) and his agenda right into the oval office. Since leading personalities behind other tech-companies, especially social networking have followed his example, first and foremost Mark Zuckerberg, announcing profound changes in their policies (Zuckerberg, 2025), which subsequently will have impact on interfaces of and culture on these platforms.

But let us take a step back and look at the broader context of these changes. 2024 being the ultimate election year (Ewe, 2023), countries have called over four billion people worldwide to cast their votes (Reece, 2024). Meanwhile political polarisation in developed countries is reaching new highs year after year since the beginning of this century (McCoy et al., 2022). The World Economic Forum (WEF) places political polarization at number three of their Global Risks Report for 2024, directly following mis- and disinformation (WEF, 2024). Especially in the bi-partisan political system of the United States, this phenomenon became very apparent in 2024, following a decade of continuous polarization (Kleinfeld, 2023).

Social media plays a crucial role in shaping opinions and fostering political education but also intensifies this political polarization through mechanisms like filter bubbles and echo chambers (Otieno, P., 2024). Platforms like X are particularly influential, with nearly 60% of users relying on it to stay informed about politics (McClain, Anderson & Gelles-Watnick, 2024).

Following McCoy & Press (2022) increasing political polarization will lead to democratic Decline eventually. The study highlights that out of 52 instances where democracies experienced pernicious polarization since 1950, half saw a significant decline in their democratic quality. Further, these polarized societies tend to be unsuccessful in their efforts to reduce polarization.

So, how can we, as designers, take action? Which role does design play in political polarization? In this essay, we will follow up on the research question, to what extent social media interfaces take effect on political polarization. We will dive deep into the design of X and suggest speculative approaches to shed light on the details of design and functionality as well as the psychological mechanics, which are embedded into the views that we interact with everyday, consuming political content or news.

Approach

To achieve this, we first have to distinguish influences by interface design on political polarization from others, such as general psychological, sociological or algorithmic effects, which all however are interconnected. Linear "For you" feeds for example are an interface decision, fueled by algorithms that curate the content you will see. Hate speech is a general problem, however, can be empowered to emotionalizing design choices.

Subsequently, we will lay out the different problem spheres that group sets of the existing features X offers, that take impact on polarization, whether positively or negatively. Those are first Engagement-centric Design, second the general Structure of Content or information architecture, third Trust and Truth and fourth Hate Speech. Inside those spheres we will analyze features twofold, once on their direct impact on polarization and once on their indirect impact. Latter could be a feature that increases engagement with the app, while engagement itself has a polarizing effect.

Engagement

Increasing engagement of your users with your product is an essential principle of the business model for the companies behind social media platforms, like X. Since most of these platforms are free to use and ad-financed, they rely on maximizing user visibility, which drives their constant pursuit of increasing the time users spend on their platforms (Center for Humane Technology, 2021). In other words, the user's attention becomes the currency with which they effectively pay these companies. This phenomenon is known as the attention economy, where our time, focus, and interactions are commodified to fuel the revenue streams of social networking companies (Burkeman, 2019).

Engagement-centric design can contain different dimensions, such as algorithm-driven strategies, however, as we want to focus on interaction design, we can cook it down to three core strategies. First and foremost, platforms can drive engagement through interactions, such as the prominent like button or comments (source). Second, notifications are key for user retention and gratification (source). Finally, the ability to scroll infinitely through feeds subtly increases time spent on these platforms (source), a topic currently at the center of public debate, as short video reel/short/tiktok feeds have entered the consumer market by storm (source).

Okay, but how does a highly engaging app affect political polarisation in discussion? A first simple but logical explanation for increased polarisation is the time spent on the platform. Those who spend more time on X, are exposed to more radical, hateful and polarized content (National Institute of Justice, 2023). At the same time those people of course interact more with posts of their peers, leading to positive feedback loops as these interactions shape their own future content, grouping users with similar opinions and thus reinforcing these of yourself. This phenomenon is called echo chambers or bubbles (source), which we will go more in depth in chapter structure of content. The third general argument for the correlation between engagement and polarisation is the fact that received engagement will drive user’s reach (X explains the mechanics here). This leads to radicalization of post content to evoke emotions in the viewers and provoke interaction, in its extreme form called engagement farming (Oestreicher, 2024).

But let’s look at these above-mentioned features of X in detail and shed light on the concrete interface design decisions that ultimately lead to an increased engagement with the platform.

Interacting with Posts

X offers a set of options that let you interact with another user’s post. Most prominently in social media, we look at “liking”, represented by a heart on X. As the names suggest, these are one-dimensional “black and white” reactions on a post, which leads to the thought of more diverse reactions. Another important functionality is reposting, formerly retweeting, with which users can share posts with or without commenting on them in their own timeline and the feeds of their peers. Last, but maybe most well known for Twitter's (back then) discussion culture, we will discuss the threaded commenting functionality and propose alternative concepts that shed light on the polarizing effects the current implementation has on the community.
